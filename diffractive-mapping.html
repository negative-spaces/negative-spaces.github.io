Diffractive mapping spatial analysis: how data comes to matter through empirical formations
abstract of presentation to be given on 4s panel Infrastructures and affective orientations of data
How are empirics made legible as data by the apparatuses that produce them? How are technoscientific and affective orientations to ‘what counts as data’ co-constitutive of an empirical account of the city? Geographic information systems (GIS) are the computational programs, desktop applications, and infrastructural hardware that facilitate the distribution, visualization, and analysis of spatial phenomenon. Coordinates append location to place, codifying the city into vector graphics and pixel grids. Vertical negotiations are rendered down and rent apart into layers like streets.shp, waterbodies.tiff, and buildings.geojson. Even when every layer is added to the screen one atop the other, their sum remains an impalpable expanse. Deep mapping is a spatial practice of situated, embodied inhabitation which assumes the physical-conceptual field of encounter as interlocutor. Empirics emerge as inextricable layerings of sensorium, affect, and infrastructure through synesthetic attunement to the entangled viscera of urban bodies, human and nonhuman. This paper performs a diffractive mapping of spatial data as it is rendered differentially legible by these two apparatuses of investigation. Diffractive mapping is not counter mapping; it is not an exercise defined by opposition so much as a praxis of accounting for how intelligibility is a product of boundary making practices, or apparatuses in the Baradian sense, and differently drawn depending on the epistemological location, positioning, and situating - that is, orientation. Drawing from practical experience, I mark how similarities and differences in the production of spatial data effect the form/at and content of research empirics.
