Deep Mapping: Thinking with place, feeling the city

While academic geographers and urban planners may theorize and design city places, urban space is performatively constituted through the everyday spatial practices of inhabitants whose navigations both cultivate and deploy practical, experiential knowledge. Drawing from Barad’s agential realism (Barad 2007), I approach the city as a physical-conceptual field whose emergent topology is continuously reconfiguring through intra-action. Framed thus, the city is not a fixed and inherently bounded object exterior to the researcher. Nor is it a field separate from the site where spatial analyses are produced, maps rendered, and urban geography written. Bringing this orientation to the ground, however, requires a praxis-based methodology for urban research that assumes the field of encounter as interlocutor. I invoke research-creation (Loveless 2019) to initiate such a dialogue with the world, asking

What could it mean to think with place? to feel the city?

I explore and respond to these questions through urban deep mapping, a practice of situated, embodied inhabitation by which I enter into dialogue with my surroundings. In what follows, I tell a story of theorizing through practice. I articulate how my theoretical framework forms and transforms in the process of navigating Vancouver. My research-creation is driven by intellectual and spatial topoi which grasp and lure me forwards even before I comprehend where they lead (Loveless 2019, 95). Pressing my chest against the Granville Bridge, I feel at once its trembling vibration and my own heart’s rapid beating. I embody a sonic superposition: vibrational waves overlap; interfere; combine. The rhythm of their resulting wave marks a pattern of interference, referred to in physics as a diffraction pattern. Visceral encounter entangles me with an infrastructure I had heretofore differentiated as an inert other––an instrument whose vantage supported an abstraction and analysis of the city below. I leave the bridge holding a little of my warmth and bring away with me this idea. . .

Deep mapping does not render down to a flat map. Yet neither does it ‘counter cartography’ (c.f. Mason-Deese 2020). As I theorize it, deep mapping is not defined by opposition so much as through iterative acts of interference with hegemonic forms of engaging/representing place, producing geographic knowledge, and rendering spatial research public. Understood this way, deep mapping is a diffractive apparatus for making interferences and studying how these patterns of difference come to matter (Barad 2007).

Many stories exist in the landscape. What do you see? The following story is but one interpretation of my ongoing conversation with my city––a response woven with support from diverse literatures read through one another. Much of what I have gleaned remains (as yet) ineffable. And, that which I have theorized evolves from my own situated spatiotemporal practice. Therefore, I do not attempt to define deep mapping so much as possibilize a mode of doing theory grounded in practice. Sharing my story enacts deep mapping as praxis: challenging the boundaries of field and research, I valorize ways of being and operating in the world (de Certeau 1984) and academy that generatively interfere with hegemonic norms of intelligibility and legibility.
